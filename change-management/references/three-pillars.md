# Three Pillars: Leading AI Change from the Front
Source: "Leading AI Change from the Front" — Directors+ Virtual Workshop Deck (March 2026)

## The Core Premise

> "You cannot guide managers through something you haven't done yourself."

When a director hasn't personally used AI tools for real work, they underestimate the learning curve, set unrealistic timelines, and can't coach through the hard parts. Their teams can tell when they're operating on secondhand information.

The phrase to internalize: **visible learner.**

---

## Pillar 1: Get Personally Fluent Before You Cascade

**What it means:**
Fluency means real reps — strategy docs, org planning, synthesizing data, prep for difficult conversations. Not watching demos. Not reading about it. Doing it, in your actual work, visibly.

**The director's job:**
Your team can see you using these tools for real work, hear you talk about what's working and what isn't, and understand this applies to all work — not just side experiments.

**Why this matters:**
Leaders set the ceiling for what feels possible. If the director hasn't personally done it, the ceiling stays where it is.

---

## Pillar 2: Facilitate the Learning Chain Yourself

**What it means:**
The cascade model: you get fluent first → you support your managers using real work from your team's context → each manager does the same with their teams. Each layer facilitates the layer below.

**The critical point:**
Not a training link. Not your most AI-enthusiastic manager running a session. **You.** When a director is in the room — learning visibly, making mistakes visible, troubleshooting in real time — it communicates something no training program can: this is real leadership work.

**What gets in the way:**
- Delegating the facilitation role ("I'll have X run a session for the team")
- Believing that sending people to TackStart is sufficient without a leader present
- Not having done enough personal reps to coach credibly

---

## Pillar 3: Make Explicit Organizational Space

**What it means:**
Without director-level cover explicitly saying *"I am authorizing you to slow down on X to invest in this"* — it doesn't happen. Teams hear the message, look at their roadmap, and there's no room. The quarter ends and adoption remains a goal that never quite got space.

**What this requires:**
- Naming specifically what gets traded off or deprioritized
- Getting alignment across peer directors — the people you need to coordinate with may be in the same room
- Understanding that unblocking teams is not a side task; it is core to leadership right now

**What leaders are expected to do:**
Identify where the friction is — psychological, technical, process-related — and actively remove it. Creating space often requires peer-to-peer coordination, not just internal team decisions.

---

## The Director's Job Right Now (Summary)

1. Get personally fluent before you cascade
2. Facilitate the learning chain yourself
3. Make explicit organizational space for experimentation

> "The plans you build are drafts. The value is that you've now thought through your specific situation at a level of specificity most planning conversations never reach. You have something real to react to, refine, and bring back to your team."

# AI Expectations at Thumbtack (2026)
Source: AI Expectations at Thumbtack — Final Document (2026)

## The Posture We're Setting

> "AI is a core capability that unlocks what Thumbtack can become, and great performance can no longer be achieved without it."

There is not a single Thumbtack role where AI should be absent from how work gets done.

**Our ambition:** Operate with the speed of a start-up half our size while delivering the output of a company twice our size.

This is not about shrinking headcount. It's about AI meaningfully expanding the horizon of what individuals and teams can do — bigger bets, faster movement, better outcomes, problems previously out of reach.

The clear expectation: **delivering impact at Thumbtack now requires using AI as a core part of how work gets done.**

---

## 4 Engagement Expectations (Everyone)

These apply regardless of role or function.

### 1. Be AI-first in consequential work
Start from the question: *"Can AI help deliver even greater impact here?"* — then pursue that answer with urgency and rigor.

This does **not** mean using AI for everything, or being an expert, or using the same approaches as everyone else. It **does** mean applying AI where it can meaningfully change outcomes like speed, quality, scale, or ambition. Using AI only for low-stakes drafts or side experiments is not sufficient.

### 2. Leverage human judgment and own outcomes
AI augments human capability; it does not replace human judgment. Individuals remain fully accountable for the outcomes of their work — quality, accuracy, ethics, impact — regardless of whether AI is used.

### 3. Stay at the AI frontier of your craft
Thumbtackers have always been expected to stay sharp. Now, that includes understanding what AI makes possible in your domain and applying it. Staying current with how AI is reshaping your craft is now part of what it means to excel in your role.

### 4. Share openly and help the organization compound learning
Achieving our ambition depends on learning that compounds across the company, not insights that stay isolated. Sharing what you learn is an expectation — even when it's uncomfortable or imperfect. This includes wins, prompts, workflows, and lessons learned, especially what didn't work.

When learning is shared consistently, individual progress becomes organizational leverage.

## The Three Learning Modes

These are not job levels or performance ratings. They are a shared language for describing how people engage with AI as they progress.

Thumbtackers should expect to embody all three over time: **operating** with AI in core work, **building** to redesign work and create leverage, and **exploring** continuously as new tools emerge.

### Explorer
Continuously explores new ways AI can be applied to work. Experiments to understand where it adds value, where it doesn't, and how it can be applied in deeper ways over time. May begin with basic tasks early in the journey — for more advanced practitioners, often involves testing increasingly complex, higher-leverage use cases.

*Examples: experimenting with new analysis techniques, testing alternative planning workflows, exploring new forms of automation, probing how AI might unlock previously infeasible work.*

**Being in Explorer mode while actively learning does not hurt performance. Remaining stagnant over time will.**

### Operator
Consistently applies AI within core, role-aligned work. Translates capability into reliable execution and measurable impact. Embeds AI into day-to-day work to improve quality, speed, clarity, and decision-making through repeatable habits and systems that compound over time.

*Examples: consistently using AI to improve throughput and quality in writing, analysis, planning, communication, decision-making, or execution by building personal systems that reduce rework and increase leverage.*

### Builder
Redesigns how work gets done by using AI to create new leverage. Goes beyond individual workflows to prototype new approaches, rethink processes, create automations or agents, and build reusable systems that unlock impact for others. May blur role boundaries in healthy ways — taking on work that was previously out of scope when doing so leads to better outcomes.

*Examples: creating automations that eliminate entire classes of manual work, redesigning team workflows around AI, building shared tools or agents, enabling others to operate at a higher level.*

## 4 Expectations for People Leaders

Leaders are expected to meet the same expectations as everyone — and to lead their teams through this moment.

### 1. Raise the Standard (Driving Results)
Leaders are accountable for making AI-enabled work the standard for how results are achieved. This includes clearly reinforcing that AI-enabled execution is expected (not optional), translating company ambition into team-level priorities and standards, and anchoring teams on more ambitious outcomes.

Teams take their cues from what leaders reinforce. If AI isn't part of how results are discussed, reviewed, and measured, it won't materially change outcomes.

### 2. Build A-Teams for This Moment (Building A Teams)
Being talent-obsessed today means building teams that can adapt, learn quickly, and compound impact through AI. Leaders are expected to hire and develop talent with AI capability and learning agility in mind, create real space for learning and experimentation within the flow of work, and identify skill or mindset gaps and close them through coaching and development.

Teams that do not evolve how they work will struggle to perform at the level Thumbtack is aiming for.

### 3. Actively Unblock and Align (Cross-Functional Collaboration)
Leaders are responsible for removing friction that prevents AI from being used effectively — whether that friction is psychological, technical, process-related, or policy-driven. This includes surfacing and escalating blockers early, sharing feedback across functions so tools and guidance improve, and collaborating to align on responsible, high-impact use cases.

Unblocking teams is not a side task. It is core to leadership in this transition.

### 4. Lead the Charge (Values-Driven Citizenship)
Leaders must be visibly at the forefront of this shift. They do not need to be the most advanced AI user — but they must be a visible learner operating with an AI-first mindset. There is no substitute for leaders using AI themselves and understanding what's possible.

This means regularly using AI in their own work to plan, think, write, analyze, or build; sharing what's working and what isn't; and role-modeling responsible use, sound judgment, and ownership of outcomes.

**Leaders set the ceiling for what feels possible. If you don't model this shift, your team won't either.**

## Manager FAQ (Key Points)

**On tradeoffs:** The shift is "redesign how work gets done," not "add AI on top." Managers own that redesign. The goal isn't compliance — it's rethinking what the work actually looks like.

**On not feeling confident enough:** You don't need to be the most advanced user. Be visibly learning, ask thoughtful questions, raise the standard of execution. That is enough to start.

**On resistance:** Engage with curiosity. Acknowledge real risks. Reinforce responsible use. Make clear that using AI where appropriate is part of the job — not a nice-to-have.

**On job anxiety:** This is the evolution of work, not the elimination of talent. The goal is accelerated impact — shipping a better Thumbtack, faster. Roles will evolve; that evolution is normal.

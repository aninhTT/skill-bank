# Visual Identity

## Brand Expression Foundation

Thumbtack's visual brand is grounded in two foundational elements: **TT Blue** and the **circle** that houses the Tackmark. These appear most often when introducing Thumbtack to new audiences.

## Circle and Photography

### Establishing Hierarchy
A single full-bleed image tells the story. The circle can contain important information and establish visual hierarchy. Rules:
- Circle should always be either TT Blue or white
- No rules on filled vs. unfilled — use what works best for the photo
- Do not use a filled circle that covers someone's face
- Do not use an unfilled circle on a busy image where it could be lost
- For unfilled circles, the stroke width should be half the text's stroke width (6-8px on social)

### Guiding the Eye
The circle can pull the viewer's eye around the page. It may be cropped, but only one circle graphic should be present at a time. Use primary colors only: white or TT Blue.

### Masking Shapes
When photography needs to share space with other content:
- **Circle** — Primary shape; best for a single hero image
- **Half circle** — When a full circle takes up too much vertical space
- **Rectangle / square** — For multiple images; use crisp 90-degree corners (no rounded corners)

Do NOT use several circles for multiple images — that creates a bubbly, playful look that is off-brand.

### Color Dip
Adding TT Blue to a masking shape brings another layer of visual branding to photography. Rules:
- TT Blue should not exceed 50% of the shape
- Blue is always in support of the photo — this is a dip, not a dunk

### Circle Don'ts
- Do not apply circles without a clear narrative or visual hierarchy
- Do not use quarter circles — only full circles, half circles, and rectangles
- Do not use secondary palette colors — TT Blue can be used in various shades
- Do not confuse circle usage with rounded corners — rectangular masks use sharp 90-degree corners

## Circle Patterns

For abstract concepts where photography feels too literal (e.g., partnership, growth), circle patterns offer a clean alternative. They can act as a primary graphic or supporting element.

### Pattern Guidelines
- Patterns should be clearly tertiary — set at hairline stroke width
- Each pattern should support a concept: Emphasis, Continuous care, Achievement, Growth
- Patterns should not feel arbitrary

### Pattern Don'ts
- Do not create patterns unconnected to a bigger idea
- Do not let patterns get too loud — they are secondary to messaging
- Do not use chunky strokes — keep at hairline width
- Do not use multiple colors — patterns stay in a single colorway

## Logo

### Primary Logo (Tack + Wordmark)
Use when people encounter Thumbtack for the first time: TV spots, out-of-home ads, first-touch marketing.
- Available in horizontal and vertical layouts
- Clear space around the logo >= the x-height of the Wordmark's "u"
- Primary version: blue Tackmark + black Wordmark, horizontal layout
- Use the horizontal two-color logo for all external communications whenever possible
- Stacked (vertical) layout only when horizontal does not fit due to space constraints
- Also available in all-black (on white backgrounds) and all-white (on footage or dark backgrounds)

### The Tack
Use when the audience already knows Thumbtack — ideally paired with copy that mentions the brand name.
- Clear space >= height of the T and the top edge of the circle
- Versions: Blue on white, white on blue, gray on white, white on footage

### Logo Applications
- **Publications**: Pair the name with the Wordmark (not the Tack) for Thumbtack-owned properties
- **Teams**: Use the Tack for internal branding. Distance between components = 1/3 of the Tack's width
- **Partnerships**: Use the Tack (not Wordmark). Match partner logo height to optical height of "T" in the Tack. Divider distance = 1/3 of Tack's width
- **Programs**: The Tack can evolve for branded programs (Thumbtack Guarantee, Top Pro badge, Pro Advisory Board). These do not replace the primary logo.

### Logo Don'ts
- Do not remove the Tack from its circle
- Do not use the Tack as typography
- Do not change the color of the Tack or Wordmark
- Do not use the brand font to create a custom wordmark
- Do not place logos over colors other than white, black, or footage
- Do not change the Wordmark color to blue

## Typography

### Thumbtack Rise
Custom brand typeface for all branded communications. Versatile sans serif. No other typeface should substitute it in external-facing materials.

**Weights:** Light, Regular, Medium, Bold, Heavy

**Weight Usage Rules:**
- Headlines: Bold or Heavy
- Subheads: Medium or Bold
- Body copy: Light or Regular
- Captions/metadata: Light

**Leading and Tracking:**
- Keep leading and tracking consistent across all applications
- Configure according to percentages to find the right balance
- General rule: the larger the font size, the tighter the tracking

**Type on Images:**
- Dark text on light imagery
- White text on dark imagery
- Use a subtle gradient over noisy images to improve contrast

**Google Applications Exception:** Use Montserrat in Google Slides, Docs, and Sheets instead of Thumbtack Rise.

### Typography Don'ts
- Do not mix type sizes within a headline
- Do not invert weight usage (e.g., Light for headlines, Heavy for body)
- Do not use secondary colors in headlines
- Do not apply drop shadows to text

## Iconography

### UI Icons
Add context alongside copy in product. Can be borrowed for marketing, but do not change the icon's original meaning. Do not use the medal icon to replace the Top Pro badge.

### Miscellaneous Icons
Seasonal or fun icons for marketing use. Make sure context is clear and they do not steal the show.

### Color Usage
Icons in secondary colors can liven up text-heavy layouts. Warning icons should be red. Otherwise, icons have no assigned colors.

### Iconography Don'ts
- Do not add container shapes around icons
- Do not use icons as illustrations, graphic elements, or patterns
- Do not replace meta icons with UI or miscellaneous icons

## Illustration

### Style
Every Thumbtack illustration is made of abstract pieces, carefully assembled into one balanced structure. Pieces build on each other to mimic the momentum of checking off to-do's.
- Ratio: 1:1 with at least 50px padding on each side
- Background: always pure white (#FFFFFF)

### Attributes
- **Monuments** — Characters build abstract pieces into impressive structures
- **Interactive** — Characters confidently create their environments; no task too big or small
- **Balanced** — Elements carefully assembled; every piece has its place
- **Grounded** — Monuments supported by a black foundation
- **Harmony** — Shapes and lines of different weights balanced with positive and negative space
- **Transparency** — Subtle color overlays create a handcrafted look (like thin pieces of paper)

### Illustration Colors
- Main: Black (#2F3033) and TT Blue (#009FD9)
- Secondary: Indigo (#5968E2)
- Accent: Yellow (#FEBE14)
- Green only for things that are actually green (trees, money)
- Add depth with different tints from the palette
- Blue tints: #3EB6E3, #99DAF0, #D1ECF7
- Indigo tints: #808BE8, #9CA4EF, #BDC3F3, #DFE1FA
- Yellow tints: #FFDD80, #FFEBB3

### Size Guidelines
| Size | Max Dimensions | Usage | Complexity |
|------|---------------|-------|------------|
| Small | 70px | Alongside copy or CTAs | 1-3 symbols, minimal detail, no characters |
| Medium | 500px | Product features, empty states, actions | 3-5 elements, no characters usually |
| Large | 180-1600px | Key product moments | Include characters (max 3 people) |

### Illustration Don'ts
- Do not change illustration colors or put them on colored backgrounds
- Do not use illustrations for abstract visual interest
- Do not crop or obscure illustrations
- Do not use illustrations to represent categories on Thumbtack

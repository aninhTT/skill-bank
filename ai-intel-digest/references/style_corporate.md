# Corporate AI Communications Style Guide

## Core Purpose
Create communications that help employees navigate AI transformation with clarity, agency, and grounded optimism. Content should be informative without being prescriptive, acknowledge legitimate concerns while orienting toward possibility, and center human judgment and craft throughout.

**Primary use case**: AI industry context briefs that translate external developments into meaningful insights for employees.

## Key Messages to Reinforce

Every piece of content should connect to one or more of these core messages:

1. **AI advances our mission**: AI helps us work faster and do things we couldn't do before, strengthening our ability to serve customers and pros better than ever.

2. **Evolution is normal**: Roles will evolve—sometimes gradually, sometimes meaningfully—and that evolution is expected in moments of technological shift. We're all shaping what the next version of our craft looks like.

3. **Human judgment is central**: AI expands what's possible, but it does not replace thinking, taste, judgment, or domain expertise. The people who thrive combine strong craft with thoughtful AI use.

4. **Learning happens by doing**: AI fluency comes from experimentation and hands-on practice. Everyone can learn this, everyone is expected to try, and we'll provide support and pathways for growth.

5. **Focus on high-value use cases**: The most meaningful impact comes from understanding workflows and applying AI where it matters most, using the tools already available.

## Tone & Voice

**Warmly informative**
- Balance clarity about organizational direction with acknowledgment of employee experience
- Be direct about change while making space for questions and concerns
- Use accessible language that respects intelligence without assuming technical expertise

**Grounded and realistic**
- Acknowledge what we know and what we're still figuring out
- Name tensions honestly: excitement and uncertainty, opportunity and disruption, speed and thoughtfulness
- Avoid both doom-and-gloom and unrealistic hype

**Positive but not performative**
- Orient toward possibility and growth without minimizing real challenges
- Express genuine belief in collective capability
- Avoid corporate platitudes or forced enthusiasm

**Employee-centered**
- Start with impact on people's day-to-day work and professional development
- Emphasize agency: what employees can shape, learn, and influence
- Connect organizational goals to individual growth and craft

## Structure for Industry Context Briefs

### 1. What's in the Headlines
Present the external developments, industry trends, or news that employees need to know about.
- Summarize key announcements, releases, or shifts in the AI landscape
- Provide enough context for employees to understand what's happening externally
- Be factual and informative without editorializing
- Group related developments together when relevant
- Use clear, accessible language to explain technical concepts

Example framing:
- "This month saw major announcements from [companies] around [capability]..."
- "The AI landscape is shifting in several notable ways..."
- "Recent developments in [area] are changing what's possible with..."

### 2. What This Means for Us
Translate external developments into implications — both for our business AND for how we work internally.

**Structure this section in two parts:**

#### For Our Business (external implications)
- Marketplace dynamics — How might this affect homeowner/pro interactions?
- Pro experience — What does this mean for service professionals?
- Customer experience — How are homeowner expectations shifting?
- Platform/partnerships — Implications for our technology and integrations

#### For How We Work (internal implications)
- Internal automation — How can we use these tools to automate our own processes?
- Security posture — What do these trends mean for organizational security?
- Team productivity — How do new tools change how our teams build and ship?
- Skills to develop — What should employees be learning or experimenting with?

Example transitions:
- "For our business, this development creates opportunities to..."
- "Internally, this shifts how we think about..."
- "For teams across the company, this means..."
- "From a security perspective, we should be thinking about..."

Consider addressing:
- How this affects our competitive position or capabilities
- What new possibilities this creates for serving customers
- How roles or workflows might evolve
- What skills or learning this suggests
- Where human judgment remains essential
- What questions we're still exploring
- What internal processes could be automated faster
- What security considerations arise from agent autonomy trends

### 3. Forward Movement
End with invitations, actions, or reflections that help employees engage with the information.
- Suggest concrete ways to experiment or learn
- Point to resources, tools, or support available
- Pose questions for continued reflection
- Emphasize collective shaping of how we use these developments
- Reinforce agency and shared responsibility

Example closings:
- "As you think about your own workflows, consider where these capabilities might apply..."
- "We're exploring how to leverage this thoughtfully—and we need your domain expertise to identify the highest-value applications"
- "The question isn't whether AI will change our work, but how we'll shape that change together"

## Language and Phrasing

**Use "we" to emphasize collective work**
- "We're navigating this together"
- "We're learning as we go"
- "We have an opportunity to shape..."
- "Together, we're discovering..."

**Frame evolution as normal and ongoing**
- "Roles evolve during technological shifts—this is expected"
- "Your job will look different in a few years, and that's part of growth"
- "We're all becoming the next version of our professional selves"

**Center human elements**
- "Your judgment matters more than ever"
- "Deep expertise is what makes AI valuable"
- "Craft and taste can't be automated"
- "Human thinking guides the technology"

**Emphasize learning by doing**
- "Fluency comes from hands-on practice"
- "Everyone can learn this through experimentation"
- "Trying is how we build capability"

**Focus on value and purpose**
- "Apply AI where it matters most"
- "Focus on workflows that serve customers"
- "Leverage tools for high-value work"

## What to Avoid

**Don't minimize or dismiss concerns**
- "There's nothing to worry about"
- "These concerns are real, and here's how we're addressing them..."

**Don't oversimplify the complexity**
- "AI will just make everything better"
- "AI creates opportunities and raises questions we're navigating together"

**Don't create false certainty**
- "Here's exactly what your job will look like in 2027"
- "We're charting this course together, learning as we go"

**Don't use corporate jargon or hype**
- "Synergizing AI capabilities to optimize outcomes"
- Use clear, direct language that respects people's intelligence

## Complexity and Nuance

**Acknowledge multiple truths simultaneously**
Use "and" logic:
- "AI will change how we work, and we're committed to supporting you through that change."
- "This technology creates new possibilities, and it also requires us to grapple with questions about bias, accuracy, and human oversight."
- "The pace of change feels fast, and taking time to learn thoughtfully is essential."

**Name specific tensions**
- Speed vs. thoughtfulness
- Automation vs. human judgment
- Efficiency gains vs. craft and quality
- Excitement about new capabilities vs. concern about job security
- Organizational direction vs. individual agency

## Citations

**Include a simple sources section at the end of each brief** that lists the newsletters and YouTube channels that informed the content. This provides transparency without obstructing the main narrative.

**Placement:** After all main content sections, separated by a clear visual break

**Format:** Simple, unobtrusive list

**Example:**

```
---

*This brief was informed by content from: The Rundown AI, The Neuron Daily, AI Adopters Club, Import AI, Montreal Ethics Brief, The Batch, plus YouTube channels including AI Explained, Matt Wolfe, and Wes Roth.*
```

**Guidelines:**
- Keep it brief and general—just list the source names
- No need for individual article citations or links
- Use conversational language: "informed by content from" or "sources included"
- Present as a single line or short paragraph at the very bottom
- Separate from main content with a horizontal rule (---) or extra spacing
- Don't make this section prominent—it's reference information, not a call to action

## Audio Script Adaptation

When converting briefs to audio scripts (~5-6 minutes / ~900 words):

**Structure (implications-heavy):**
- **Intro (30 sec):** Date, quick preview
- **Part 1 — The News (~2 min):** Cover the key stories with enough detail to understand what happened
- **Part 2 — What This Means (~3 min):** The bulk of the episode. Business implications (customers, marketplace, pros) + internal implications (automation, security, tools, skills)
- **Outro (30 sec):** One action item or reflection, sign-off

**Key principle:** The news provides context, but the real value is what it means for us — for our customers and for how we work. Spend more time on implications than on reporting.

**Tone: Casual and relaxed**
- Talk like you're catching up with a colleague over coffee, not presenting to executives
- Use contractions freely: "it's," "we're," "that's," "don't"
- Drop the corporate voice — "pretty wild," "honestly," "here's the thing"
- React naturally: "This one's interesting..." / "OK so this is kind of wild..."
- Short, punchy sentences. Fragments are fine.
- It's OK to editorialize a bit — share genuine reactions, not just facts
- Cover both business implications AND internal implications (security, automation, team tools)

**Casual transition examples:**
- "Alright, let's get into it."
- "So here's what happened..."
- "OK, now the interesting part — what does this mean for us?"
- "Honestly, this one caught my attention because..."
- "The takeaway here is pretty simple..."

**What to avoid:**
- Stiff corporate phrasing ("It is important to note that...")
- Overly formal transitions ("Moving on to our next topic...")
- Reading like a press release
